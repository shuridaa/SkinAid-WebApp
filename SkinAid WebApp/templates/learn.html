This page offers helpful information on the skin diseases SkinAid may detect. Always consult a healthcare professional for any medical concerns.

What is it? A rough, scaly patch caused by long-term sun exposure. These are precancerous and can develop into squamous cell carcinoma.

Why it happens: Long-term UV damage from sunlight or tanning beds.

What you can do: Use sunscreen, avoid direct sun, and see a dermatologist if it persists.

ðŸ”¹ Basal Cell Carcinoma

What is it? A slow-growing skin cancer that looks like a pearly or waxy bump. Usually treatable.

Why it happens: DNA damage from UV exposure over time.

What you can do: Get it checked early. May require minor surgery. Use sunscreen regularly.

ðŸ”¹ Benign Keratosis

What is it? Non-cancerous skin growths like age spots or seborrheic keratosis.

Why it happens: Age, sun exposure, and genetics.

What you can do: Usually harmless. Monitor for changes. Can be removed cosmetically.

ðŸ”¹ Dermatofibroma

What is it? A small, firm bump that may follow a minor skin injury. Typically harmless.

Why it happens: Often develops after insect bites or shaving cuts.

What you can do: No treatment usually needed. Remove if painful or bothersome.

ðŸ”¹ Melanoma

What is it? A dangerous form of skin cancer. Early detection is critical.

Why it happens: UV damage and genetic factors cause skin cell mutation.

What you can do: Check moles for asymmetry, border, color changes, and size. See a specialist immediately.

ðŸ”¹ Melanocytic Nevus

What is it? Common moles. Typically harmless unless they change.

Why it happens: Caused by clusters of pigment cells; influenced by genetics and sun exposure.

What you can do: Monitor for size, shape, or color changes. Regular skin checks are recommended.
